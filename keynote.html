Keynote Speech 1



The Many Faces of Ultra-wideband Concurrent Transmissions

Speaker: Gian Pietro Picco, University of Trento

Abstract:

The conventional wisdom of wireless networking tells us that concurrent transmissions on the same channel from multiple senders colliding at a receiver result in packet loss. However, this is not always true: when transmissions are tightly synchronized, and given power conditions hold, one of the colliding transmissions is nearly always received due to the capture effect. In this talk, we explore the opportunities enabled by this technique in ultra-wideband (UWB) radios, thanks to their unique characteristics. On one hand, concurrent transmissions can be exploited for multi-hop communication, enabling novel network primitives and protocol designs with unprecedented performance and reliability, including network-wide time synchronization with an error of less than 30ns. On the other hand, they can be exploited in conjunction with the distinctive features of UWB: distance estimation and localization. Indeed, UWB transmissions rely on very short pulses; their individual times of arrival can be discriminated in the channel impulse response (CIR) at the receiver. However, the ns-level resolution required for localization poses significant challenges in accurately extracting time information. We report our experience in designing unconventional schemes where a device can simultaneously estimate its distance from several others, or a set of devices can localize countless targets in an area, yielding accuracy comparable to conventional schemes but significantly faster and more energy-efficient operation.

Bio:

Gian Pietro Picco is a professor in the Department of Information Engineering and Computer Science (DISI) at the University of Trento, Italy. His research has spanned software engineering, middleware, and distributed systems, and is currently focused on low-power wireless networking and localization for the Internet of Things and cyber-physical systems. The research performed in his group emphasizes the validation of novel approaches and protocols via system prototypes and their evaluation in real-world testbeds and applications. He is the recipient of several awards, including a "Most Influential Paper" at ICSE'07 for a paper published a decade earlier, and Best Paper Awards at IPSN (2009, 2011, 2015, 2023), PerCom (2012), EWSN (2018), and IPIN (2019). He has served as General Chair and Program Chair for several flagship conferences (e.g., SenSys, CPS-IoT Week, SECON, EWSN, IoTDI, DCOSS, Middleware). He is an associate editor for ACM Trans. on Sensor Networks (TOSN) and has served in the same role for IEEE Trans. on Software Engineering (TSE) and the J. of Pervasive and Mobile Computing. He was also the founding Editor-in-Chief of the ACM Trans. on the Internet of Things (TIOT).

Keynote Speech 2



System-Level Design and Integration Strategies for Multi-Modal Robotic Locomotion

Speaker: Pei-Chun Lin, National Taiwan University

Abstract:

Robotic systems are inherently complex, comprising spatial elements such as mechanical structures, mechanisms, and mechatronic assemblies, as well as temporal computational processes including perception, decision-making, and actuation. Achieving versatile and agile behaviors in robotic platforms to meet the demands of diverse operational scenarios necessitates a holistic and well-orchestrated approach to design and integration. This keynote will present recent developments in robotic systems, with a particular emphasis on legged and leg-wheel hybrid locomotion platforms.

Drawing inspiration from the dynamic mobility of legged animals, which exhibit remarkable adaptability to unstructured and uneven terrains, this work explores the biomechanical and control principles that inform the design and locomotion strategies of legged robots. In contrast, wheeled locomotion offers advantages in smoothness and energy efficiency, representing one of the most enduring and effective forms of engineered mobility. By integrating these complementary modalities, leg-wheel hybrid robots offer a promising synthesis of adaptability and efficiency.

The presentation will address both the design methodologies and control frameworks essential for realizing such multi-modal robotic systems. Emphasis will be placed on system-level integration techniques that enable seamless coordination of mechanical and computational subsystems to achieve robust, agile, and task-flexible robotic behavior.

Bio:

Pei-Chun Lin is a Distinguished Professor in the Department of Mechanical Engineering and Associate Dean in the College of Engineering at National Taiwan University (NTU), Taipei, Taiwan. He is the Chairman of the Robotics Society of Taiwan (RST) and the director of the Bio-inspired Robotics Laboratory (BioRoLa) at NTU. His research interests include robotics, mechanical design, sensor design/fusion, control, locomotion, and robotic applications.

Pei-Chun Lin received B.S. and M.S. degrees in mechanical engineering from NTU in 1996 and 1998, respectively, as well as an M.S. degree in electrical engineering and computer science and a Ph.D. in mechanical engineering from the University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, U.S.A. in 2005. He then worked as a postdoctoral research fellow in the Department of Materials Science and Engineering at the University of Pennsylvania before moving to NTU in 2007. He served as an associate editor of IEEE Transactions on Robotics between 2016 and 2020 and a technical editor of IEEE/ASME Transactions on Mechatronics between 2021 and 2024, and he is currently an associate editor of IEEE Robotics and Automation Letters. He received the Ta-You Wu Memorial Award, FutureTech Breakthrough Award, and Outstanding Research Award from the National Science and Technology Council, Taiwan. He also received the Distinguished Teaching Awards from NTU, Institute of Engineering Education, Taiwan (IEET), and Taiwan Society of Precision Engineering (TSPE). He is a Fellow of The American Society of Mechanical Engineers, USA (ASME), The Institute of Engineering and Technology, UK (IET), The Robotics Society of Taiwan (RST), and The Society of Theoretical and Applied Mechanics of the Republic of China (STAM).

Reflections Talk



Reflections on Real-Time Embedded Systems: From Foundational Breakthroughs to Embodied Intelligence

Speaker: Tulika Mitra, National University of Singapore

Abstract:

Over the past decades, real-time embedded systems have evolved from simple micro-controllers to foundational technologies forming the invisible backbone of our modern world. They now span applications from industrial automation to everyday consumer electronics. Pioneering breakthroughs such as real-time scheduling, WCET analysis, model-based co-design, and formally verified kernels have enabled the seamless integration of physical and cyber domains. Asia, in particular, has emerged as a major centre of academic excellence, technological innovation, and large-scale deployment, exemplified by Singapore's ambitious Smart Nation initiative.

Today, we stand at the cusp of another transformation: embodied intelligence, powered by on-device AI. Embedded systems research has consistently anticipated such frontiers, driving innovations that reshape broader technological trends. This emerging paradigm imposes stringent constraints in latency, real-time responsiveness, ultra-low power consumption, and robust safety and security guarantees. Our community is not only well-positioned to meet these demands but to define the next era of intelligent systems. In this talk, I will reflect on the key milestones that brought us to this point and frame the research directions necessary to realize the promise of embodied intelligence.

Bio:

Tulika Mitra is Provost’s Chair Professor of Computer Science and Vice-Provost (Academic Affairs) at the National University of Singapore (NUS). Her research focuses on the design automation of smart, energy-efficient, safety-critical embedded computing systems. Her work is characterized by a systems-centric approach spanning hardware and software to advance accelerator design, power/thermal management in heterogeneous computing systems, and WCET analysis of real-time systems. She has served as the Editor-in-Chief of ACM Transactions on Embedded Computing Systems, General/Program Chair of ICCAD, and General Chair of ESWEEK. Tulika has been recognized with the Embedded Systems Week (ESWEEK) Test-of-Time Award, several best paper award or candidates in top conferences, Indian Institute of Science S. K. Chatterjee Award for Outstanding Woman Researcher, and ACM SIGDA Distinguished Service Award among others.
